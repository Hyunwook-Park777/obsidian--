abstract

Increase the equivalence ratio is a good way to improve performance of turbocharged
hydrogen engines at low engine speeds. To explore the feasibility of this strategy, this
paper investigated the experimental data of a 2.3 L turbocharged port fuel injection (PFI)
hydrogen engine at 1500 rpm and 2000 rpm. The results showed that the power can increase
from 6.8 kW to 21 kW at 2000 rpm and from 6.4 kW to 16.5 kW at 1500 rpm with
increasing of the equivalence ratio. However, the equivalence ratio corroding to the biggest
power is 0.8 at 1500 rpm and 0.9 at 2000 rpm because the turbocharged pressure and the
volumetric efficiency at 2000 rpm are higher than the ones at 1500 rpm. The biggest BTE
can reach to 30.1% at 2000 rpm and 29.3% at 1500 rpm within the range of 0.65e0.8. The
covariance of indicated mean effective pressure (CoVimep) of turbocharged hydrogen is
lower than 1.5% at low engine speeds and the combustion stability increased with the
increase of equivalence ratio. The NOx can be reduced from 877 ppm to 0 ppm at 1500 rpm
and from 1259 ppm to 17 ppm at 2000 rpm, which means the reduction efficiency of
H2þTWC can exceed 99%.

Introduction

Global warming is the most serious environmental issue in
the world and the biggest challenge for traditional energy. The
carbon dioxide (CO2) is the main part of the greenhouse and
most of the CO2 comes from the consumption of traditional
energy [1]. To control the effect of greenhouse, the target of
COP21 meeting is to keep global warming below 2 C
in Pairs in

2015. The internal combustion engine is an important way of

energy consumption and many countries begin to control the
CO2 emission through the fuel consumption regulations. For
example, the target of American is 4.32 L/km before 2025,
Europe is 3.8 L/km before 2020, Japan is 4.92 L/km before 2020,
China is 5 L/km before 2020 [2]. These regulations promote the
development of internal combustion engines into two directions:
on the one hand, the development of cleaner and
more efficient conventional fuel internal combustion engines;
on the other hand, the use of alternative fuels. Hydrogen has
the characteristics of fast combustion speed, low ignition

internal combustion engines [3e5].

The mixture formation of a hydrogen fuel internal combustion
engine is generally divided into two types: PFI and
direct injection (DI). The advantage of DI is that the gaseous
fuel is injected after the intake valve is closed, which does not

occupy the cylinder volume, so that the power of the hydrogen
internal combustion engine is close to that of the same
displacement gasoline engine. Thomas Wallner et al. conducted
tests in a 0.5 L, DI hydrogen engine and the results
showed it can maintain the same power as comparable gasoline
engines [6]. Alan Welch et al. compared the brake mean
effective pressure (BMEP) of PFI hydrogen engine, gasoline
engine and DI hydrogen engine and they found that the
theoretical BMEP of DI is 1.17 times that of the gasoline engine
[7]. Another advantage is that DI hydrogen engine is benefit to

the combustion because it can achieve stratified combustion
through the multiple injection strategy [8e10]. However, the
way of DI has two problems, which limit the utilizing of DI. On
the one hand, the nozzle reliability is low because the
hydrogen nozzle cannot be cooled and lubricated like the oil
nozzle, which will increase the possibility of the burnt out or
deformation for the nozzle [11]. On the other hand, the
hydrogen nozzle needs higher injection pressure than the
other fuel because of minimal of hydrogen, which leads to the

poor sealing. For example, Kimitaka Yamane et al. used a new
material of O ring with the hardness of HS 94 with no backup
ring and they found the hydrogen nozzle can operate 700 h for
the durability test and 100 h for the DI hydrogen engine [12].
The nozzle of PFI can increase the reliability because of the
lower injection pressure and the lower temperature than the
DI nozzle. The way PFI can avoid the two problems.

However, the power of PFI hydrogen engine is less than the
comparable gasoline engine because the fuel will occupy the
cylinder volume and the heat value of hydrogen-air at stoichiometric
ratio is 3.18 MJ/m3
, which is smaller than the

3.81 MJ/m3 of gasoline-air. Thus, many researchers explored
the effect of boosting the intake air pressure on the performance
of PFI hydrogen engine. Ford Groups investigated two
kinds of PFI boosting hydrogen engines and their results
showed that the torque can be equal to the one of the compressed
natural gas engines at the same volume [13,14]. They
also introduced a 4.0 L PFI turbocharged hydrogen engine and
the maximum BTE can reach to 35.5% [15]. BMW Groups reported
a 0.5 L PFI hydrogen engine and the intake air pressure
were boosted to 0.185 MPa [16]. They showed that the BMEP
can reach to 1.8 MPa at all working conditions through
adjusting the equivalence ratio. Verhelst et al. developed a
7.4 L PFI supercharged hydrogen engine [17]. They found the

maximum torque can increase 60% than the one of naturally
aspirated and the power can increase 30% than the one of the
basic gasoline engine. Moreover, they found the way of stoichiometric
ratio adding with exhaust gas recirculation (EGR)
can get higher BTE than the way of lean equivalence ratio [18].
Das et al. [19] presented a 2.5 L turbocharged hydrogen engine
with an intercooler, whose power can reach to 66.7 kW at

3200 rpm. Boosting pressure didn't only increase the power,
the NOx emission also increased. Mover, the torque at low
engine speed and high load also decreased because the

exhaust gas cannot afford enough energy to make the turbine
work, which will decrease the intake air mass. To improve the

performance at low engine speeds, increasing equivalence
ratio is an important way because the high equivalence ratio
can increase the energy into the cylinder [20].

Moreover, cycle variation is another important parameter
to evaluate the performance of hydrogen engine. Kim et al.

studied the effects of engine speed, hydrogen injection timing,
equivalence ratio and ignition timing on the cyclic variation of
DI hydrogen internal combustion engine. The results showed
that COVimep is suitable for the cyclic variation of characterization
of combustion in DI hydrogen engine [21]. Subramanian
et al. explored the cycle variation evaluation

parameters of PFI hydrogen engine and found that increasing
the equivalence ratio can reduce the cyclic variation [22].
Similar test conclusions were also obtained under lean conditions
by Ji Changwei et al. [23]. Chen Yu studied the evaluation
method of the cycle variation of hydrogen engine at the
idle condition and analyzed the correlation between the
maximum cylinder pressure and the corresponding three

values of the crank angle with the average effective pressure.
The results showed that the COVimep is an ideal choose under
idle condition [24]. In the past work of our research group, we
have conducted the test at a 2.0 L naturally aspirated PFI
hydrogen engine and found that the COVimep is the best
evaluation parameter to the PFI hydrogen engine at all working
conditions [25]. However, the cycle variation of turbocharged
PFI hydrogen at low engine speeds is different the
above results because the turbine will increase the back
pressure, which will deteriorate exchanging process and increase
the exhaust gas residual coefficient. The result is that
the cycle variation will increase at low engine speeds for
turbocharged PFI hydrogen engine. Thus, it is necessary to
explore the effect of equivalence ratio on the cycle variation
and propose strategy to increase the combustion stability.

The NOx emission of turbocharged hydrogen engine will
increase because the high combustion temperature. The results
of reference [19] showed that the NOx emission was over

800 ppm though the equivalence ratio was controlled smaller
than 0.6. Verhelst et al. proposed the solutions of EGR þ TWC
to control the NOx emission and the result showed the TWC
conversion efficiencies can achieve more than 99% [18]. Ford
group also adopted the solution of EGR þ TWC [26]. Sierens
et al. compared the lean and cold EGR ways on the CFR engine
[27]. They found that EGR reduced the pumping loss at low
load and the heat transfer loss also decreased. These solutions
can decrease the NOx emission but there are two problems: 1)
the heat capacity of water is smaller than the one of CO2,
which will drop the effect of EGR on the NOx; 2) the cooled EGR
will product liquid water, which will corrode the EGR system;
3) the high temperature exhaust gas increase the possibility of
the abnormal combustion-backfire. Thus, it is necessary to
explore new strategy to solve the NOx emission for turbocharged
PFI hydrogen engine under the premise that other
performance can meet the requirements.

improve the performance of turbocharged hydrogen engine at
low engine speeds. To explore the effect of increasing equivalence
ratio on the performance, Section Effect of equivalence

Material and method

A 2.3 L, 4 St, PFI hydrogen engine with a turbocharger was
installed into a hydrogen engine test bench. The parameters
of the turbocharged hydrogen engine are shown as Table 1
and the sketch is described as Fig. 1.

This paper mainly explored the effect of equivalence ratio
(l) on the power, BTE and NOx controlling strategy for the
turbocharged hydrogen engine at low engine speeds, so
different working conditions of two engine speeds (1500 rpm
and 2000 rpm) and l ¼ 0.4e1.1 were conducted in the test
bench. To absorb and motor the turbocharged hydrogen engine,
a CW250 eddy current dynamometer was installed. The
oil and water cooling system, intercooler system were also
installed to control the temperature of the hydrogen engine.
The electric control unit (ECU) was based on the PROtronic

system and the Simulink mode of the Matlab. The controlling
strategies can be achieved by the ECU system. The data
measure system mainly included the air flow meter, hydrogen
flow meter (CMF010 and CMF025), temperature (Pt100 and K
type thermocouple), pressure of inlet and outlet pipe (steady
state and transient pressure sensors), environment temperature
and pressure, the concentration and component of the
exhaust gas (AVL DiGas 4000 Light). Moreover, a combustion
analyzer of Kistler was also used in the test to measure the
combustion process at different working conditions. Before
conducting the test, the hydrogen engine should be warmed
up to the suitable temperature, which the oil temperature was
90 C
and the temperature of the cooling water was 80 C.
Due
to the limitation of the mass of hydrogen nozzle and knock
[26], the throttle angle was set to 17deg at 1500 rpm and 16deg
at 2000 rpm. The NOx emission at front and behind of TWC
was measured at the same working condition using the AVL
DiGas 4000 Light.

Results and discussions

Effect of equivalence ratio on the power and BTE at low
engine speed

Equivalence ratio can increase the combustion velocity, which
has effect on the power and BTE. Fig. 2 shows the power ascends
with increasing of the equivalence ratio at 1500 rpm and
2000 rpm. As the equivalence ratio changes from 0.4 to 0.9, the
power can increase from 6.8 kW to 21 kW at 2000 rpm while
the power decreases after l > 0.9. The reason can be explained
by the descending of air mass flow when the equivalence ratio
is bigger than 0.9 as Fig. 3 shown. With the increase of the
equivalence ratio, hydrogen occupies more cylinder volume,
which leads to the decrease of the air flow mass when the
throttle opening angle keeps constant. Compared the curve of
2000 rpm with 1500 rpm in Fig. 2, the maximum of the air flow
mass achieves at l z 0.8 at 1500 rpm. The difference between
1500 rpm and 2000 rpm can be found in Fig. 4. The turbocharged
pressure at 2000 rpm is bigger than the one at
1500 rpm, which will raise the volumetric efficiency and lead
to the increase of the air flow mass.

The effect of equivalence ratio on the BTE can be found as
Fig. 5 shown. BTE increases when l < 0.8 and decreases when
l > 0.8 at 1500 rpm and 2000 rpm. The reason can be explained
from two aspects: on the one hand, the hydrogen flow mass
increases from 0.75 kg/h to 1.92 kg/h at 1500 rpm and from
0.85 kg/h to 2.49 kg/h at 2000 rpm as Fig. 6 shown, which
means more energy into the cylinder and the higher load. The
larger load can increase the mechanical efficiency as Fig. 7
shown, which the mechanical efficiency increases from 73%
to 92% at 1500 rpm and from 63% to 92%. The higher mechanical
efficiency means the larger BTE. On the other hand,
the combustion velocity ascends rapidly with increase of the
equivalence ratio, which shortens combustion duration. As
Fig. 8 shown, the combustion duration (10%e90%) decreases
from 25.4CA
to 5.1CA
at 1500 rpm and from 25.4CA
to 6.1CAat
2000 rpm. These are the two factors that induce the increase
of BTE when l < 0.8. When the equivalence ratio is larger than
0.8, the effect of combustion velocity and loads decreases and
more energy is taken away by the exhaust gas and cooling
water [18]. The result leads to the decrease of the BTE. Moreover,
the indicated thermal efficiency (ITE) decreases from
35.3% to 27.4% at 1500 rpm and from 36.7% to 27.2% at
2000 rpm as Fig. 8 shown. Thus, increasing the equivalence
ratio can raise the torque and increase the BTE within a
certain equivalence ratio ranges as Fig. 9 shown.

Effect of equivalence ratio on the NOx and controlling
strategy at low engine speed

Increasing power and reducing NOx emission are the mainly
contradictions for the hydrogen engines [28,29]. Increasing
equivalence ratio can raise the power and BTE at low engine
speeds, so this section will explore the effect of equivalence
ratio on the NOx emission and put forward a strategy to

improve the comprehensive performance of turbocharged
hydrogen engine at low engine speeds. In the pure hydrogen
internal combustion engines, the main component of NOx is
NO and its formation satisfies the Zeldovich mechanism:
temperature, oxygen concentration and action time. The
tendency of NOx emission changing with equivalence ratio
has been described in the reference [30], so this paper introduce
the difference at low engine speeds. As Fig. 14 shown,
when the equivalence ratio increases from 0.6 to 0.8, the NOx
emission at 1500 rpm is higher than the one at 2000 rpm. The
reason is that the oxygen concentration and combustion
temperature are roughly equal but the action time at 1500 rpm
is longer than the one at 2000 rpm. The temperature in the
TWC also is shown as Fig. 14(b).

Hydrogen is the best reductant and many researchers have
focused on the utilizing it to reduce the NOx emission such as
H2-SCR. Hydrogen as the fuel for the hydrogen engines can be
gained more easily than the other fuel engines, which can

remove the equipment of producing or carrying hydrogen.
Moreover, TWC is the cheapest and most conventional catalytic
device on modern vehicles. Thus, this paper puts forward
the strategy of reducing NOx emission using H2-TWC and the
equivalence ratio of slightly higher than stoichiometric ratio.
In order to explore the feasibility of the strategy, the NOx

emission at front and behind of TWC changing with equivalence
ratios has been showed as Fig. 15 and Fig. 16 at 1500 rpm,
Th ¼ 17deg, MBT and 2000 rpm, Th ¼ 16deg, MBT. The NOx

emission at behind of TWC is less than the one at front of TWC
when the equivalence ratio is smaller than 0.95 at 1500 rpm

and 2000 rpm. There are two factors to explain this phenomenon:
on the one hand, few lubricating oil will be burned at
operating the hydrogen engines, which produce the HC and
CO. These HC and CO can reduce the NOx emission in the
TWC. On the other hand, there is still a small amount of
hydrogen in the exhaust gas when the equivalence ratio is
smaller than 1, which has been proved by the Ford groups [17].
Thus, the HC, CO and H2 can reduce the NOx emission in the
TWC even the exhaust gas has high oxygen concentration but
the amount of reduced NOx emission is a bit little due to few

reductant and low conversion efficiency. The NOx emission
behind TWC rapidly decrease to zero when l > 0.95. The
reason is that the oxygen concentration declines to zero and
more H2 can be used to reduce the NOx emission in the TWC.

Conclusions

Improving the performance at low engine speeds for the PFI
turbocharged hydrogen engine means increasing the power,
enhancing BTE and controlling the NOx emission. This paper

puts forward the strategy of H2þTWC and the equivalence
ratio slightly higher than stoichiometric ratio to improve the
comprehensive performance. Based on the above results,
some conclusions can be got as following.

(1) The hydrogen flow mass can block the increase of air
flow mass at high equivalence ratio when the throttle
opening angle keeps constant for PFI hydrogen engines
because the total flow mass into the cylinder is constant.
The equivalence ratio corroding to the maximum
power is 0.8 at 1500 rpm and 0.9 at 2000 rpm, which is
affected by the volumetric efficiency and turbocharged
pressure. Thus, choosing the suit equivalence ratio can
increase both the power and BTE when the volumetric
efficiency is constant.

(2) Increasing equivalence ratio cannot only ascend the
power, but the BTE also increases within a certain
equivalence ratio ranges (such as 0.6e0.8) because the
equivalence ratio can speed up the combustion velocity
and improve the gas exchange process at low engine
speeds of turbocharged hydrogen engines.

(3) The cycle number more than 150 can ensure that the
variation amplitude of cumulative average is smaller
than 0.3% for turbocharged hydrogen engines at low
engine speeds. The COVimep of turbocharged hydrogen
is lower than 1.5% and decrease with increase of the
equivalence ratio.

(4) The NOx emission can be reduced by the HC, CO and H2
under oxygen-rich environment but the conversion efficiency
is very low. When the equivalence ratio is
bigger than 0.95, the NOx emission at behind of TWC
presents a cliff-like decline and the value is close to
0 because the oxygen concentration decreases and the
H2 can be used to reduce NOx in the TWC.
